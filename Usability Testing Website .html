Home 🏠

Welcome to Dylan Lu’s comprehensive usability testing project for Twitch, the world’s leading live-streaming platform with over 240 million monthly active users. Twitch enables real-time video broadcasting, interactive chat, and community engagement through follows, subscriptions, and virtual currency (Bits).

Why Twitch? With its rich media interface, diverse content categories (Gaming, Music, Creative, IRL), and complex interaction flows (autoplay, chat gating, subscription models), Twitch presents unique usability challenges. Improving even small UX elements on Twitch can impact millions of users.

Heuristic Evaluation 🔍

Our expert review against Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics not only identified violations but also contrasted each issue against ideal best practices. By systematically examining Twitch’s interface, we pinpointed areas where the design both aids and hinders users:

  • 1. Visibility of System Status
    Issue: The search field is concealed behind a small magnifier icon, leaving users guessing whether typing is possible until they click.
    Impact: Newcomers hesitate to explore or abandon tasks when uncertain how to initiate search.
    Best Practice: An always-visible search input with placeholder text (Search games, channels…) provides instant affordance and feedback.
  • 2. Match Between System and the Real World
    Issue: The Subscribe button suggests a free action (subscription/follow) but actually triggers payment. The Follow icon (heart) is less prominent.
    Impact: Users confuse free and paid actions, leading to surprise or hesitation.
    Best Practice: Clearly label actions, e.g., Subscribe (Paid) and Follow (Free), with explanatory tooltips or an onboarding overlay.
  • 3. User Control & Freedom
    Issue: Once deep in channel pages or VOD lists, there is no breadcrumb trail or visible “Back” link beyond the browser back button.
    Impact: Users feel trapped or disoriented when they want to return to previous context.
    Best Practice: Add a persistent breadcrumb navigation or a fixed “Home” icon to restore orientation.
  • 4. Consistency & Standards
    Issue: Terminology and icon design vary across sections (e.g., “Home,” “Browse,” “Explore”).
    Impact: Inconsistent labels increase cognitive load as users relearn navigation in different areas.
    Best Practice: Adopt uniform terms and iconography (e.g., always “Browse” for category menus) and maintain style consistency.
  • 5. Error Prevention
    Issue: Clicking the chat box immediately prompts a login wall with no prior notification.
    Impact: Interrupts task flow; users may feel the site “broke.”
    Best Practice: Warn users in advance that chat requires login, or allow a brief guest-preview mode.
  • 6. Recognition Rather Than Recall
    Issue: Platform-specific terms like “Bits” or “Raids” appear without explanations.
    Impact: Users must recall or guess meanings, leading to confusion.
    Best Practice: Provide inline definitions or a quick reference overlay for jargon.
  • 7. Flexibility & Efficiency of Use
    Issue: No keyboard shortcuts or quick-access features for power users.
    Impact: Experienced users cannot accelerate repetitive tasks.
    Best Practice: Implement shortcuts (e.g., “/” focuses search, “V” opens VODs) and allow saving favorite filters.
  • 8. Aesthetic & Minimalist Design
    Issue: The homepage displays multiple carousels, live tiles, sidebars, and pop-ups simultaneously.
    Impact: First-time visitors are overwhelmed by visual clutter and don’t know where to look.
    Best Practice: Simplify the default homepage for new users—feature a limited set of sections and enable progressive disclosure of advanced elements.
  • 9. Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, & Recover from Errors
    Issue: Generic error messages (e.g., stream playback failures) offer no guidance on next steps.
    Impact: Users cannot self-recover and may abandon the task.
    Best Practice: Provide actionable error messages with suggestions (“Reload stream,” “Return to homepage”).
  • 10. Help & Documentation
    Issue: Support and FAQ links are buried in the footer, disconnected from interactive elements.
    Impact: Users struggling with tasks cannot quickly access help.
    Best Practice: Embed contextual “?” icons or “Need help?” links adjacent to complex features, opening concise in-line guidance.

Personas 👥

  • Overwhelmed Newcomer: First-time visitor; web-savvy but unfamiliar with live-stream layouts; goals: find specific streams quickly; pain points: autoplay, hidden navigation.
  • Methodical Explorer: Occasional viewer seeking targeted content (Music, IRL, Creative); goals: filter by category, quick previews; pain points: unclear tags, information overload.
  • Semi-Skilled User: Regular Twitch visitor comfortable with basics; goals: efficient VOD access, community engagement; pain points: advanced features (Prime subs, VOD filters) hidden.

Scenarios 📖

Based on our three participants' sessions, we defined three core scenarios illustrating distinct user journeys through Twitch’s interface:

1. First‑Time Stream Discovery

User: Mahani Bahari
Context: Completely new to Twitch (“I didn’t even touch anything, yet a video was already playing”).
Goal: Quickly find and watch a live Minecraft stream.
Key Steps:

  1. Encounter the autoplay; express confusion and attempt to pause or navigate away.
  2. Spot the dual search icons; hesitate over which to use.
  3. Type “Minecraft” into the search bar; interpret dropdown suggestions and select a stream.
  4. Review channel info; consider following but uncertain due to unclear labels.

2. Category Exploration & Interest Selection

User: Caitlin DelVillano
Context: Novice streamer viewer familiar with YouTube Live and TikTok.
Goal: Discover and compare non‑gaming categories (e.g., Music, Creative).
Key Steps:

  1. Scan top navigation and drop‑down; note presence of generic categories (“I like that they have basic categories”).
  2. Click “Browse” and explore subdivisions under chosen category.
  3. Switch to search for “Minecraft” to contrast browsing vs direct search.
  4. Reflect on content length preferences: short highlights vs full live streams.

3. Semi‑Skilled Engagement & VOD Navigation

User: Seth Gundlach
Context: Occasional Twitch user, comfortable with streaming basics.
Goal: Support a streamer via follow/subscribe, use chat, and locate past broadcasts.
Key Steps:

  1. Navigate via left “Browse” link and search bar; comment on autoplay distraction and input placeholders.
  2. Select a Minecraft live stream; evaluate playback smoothness and UI controls.
  3. Click “Follow” (expecting a seamless flow for signed‑in users).
  4. Open the “Videos” tab; note difficulty filtering past broadcasts by date or topic.

Moderator Script 🎙️

Enrollment text and key prompts synthesized from transcripts:

  1. Opening & Consent:

    “Hello [Name], thank you for helping test Twitch today. I’m Dylan. This is a usability study about the site, not you. May I record your screen and audio?”

  2. Warm-Up & Background:

    “How are you today? Have you used any streaming platforms like YouTube Live, TikTok, or Netflix? How often?”

  3. Think-Aloud Instruction:

    “Please narrate your thoughts as you go—for example, ‘I’m looking for the search bar because…’. I’ll remind you if you go silent.”

  4. Task 1 – Homepage Exploration:

    “Scroll the homepage and describe anything confusing or surprising, such as autoplay behavior, layout, or hidden options.”

  5. Task 2 – Section Interest:

    “Which category (Games, Music, IRL, Creative) would you explore first? Why?”

  6. Task 3 – Search Process:

    “Use the search bar to look for ‘Minecraft’. Describe each step: locating, typing, interpreting dropdowns, and selecting.”

  7. Task 4 – Live & VOD Navigation:

    “Play the live stream you selected. Then find and open the ‘Videos’ or ‘Past Broadcasts’ tab to view recorded content. What helps or hinders you?”

  8. Task 5 – Engagement Flow:

    “Attempt to follow/subscribe to the channel and send a chat message. Note any prompts, errors, or unexpected fees.”

  9. Debrief & Closing:

    “Thank you! On a scale of 1 (very difficult) to 5 (very easy), please rate each task. What was most confusing? Any suggestions? Would you return?”

Participant Summaries

  • Mahani Bahari: Found the site extremely unfriendly, overwhelmed by autoplay, hidden navigation, and unclear icons; unlikely to return without guidance.
  • Caitlin DelVillano: Appreciated responsive search but struggled with cluttered interface and vague labels; suggested better tagging and concise previews.
  • Seth Gundlach: Experienced semi-skilled user; noted distracting autoplay and dual search bars confusion, recommended clear input placeholders and single search entry.
  • Power User: Navigated smoothly, but advanced filters and VOD search need improvement; endorsed optional onboarding overlays for newcomers.

Summary 📋

  • Discoverability: New and semi-skilled users struggled to locate search and category filters; clear, visible search bar is essential.
  • Clarity: Ambiguous labels (“Subscribe” vs “Follow”) and dual search bars confused participants; explicit labeling and tooltips recommended.
  • Onboarding: Dense homepage overwhelms first-time visitors; a dismissible walkthrough can highlight core features.
  • Content Filtering: Participants desire tagging, preview length indicators, and channel-specific search to narrow down results effectively.
  • Engagement Flows: Chat gating and subscription processes lack preemptive guidance; in-context prompts can ease the flow.
  • VOD Access: Past broadcasts hidden in deep navigation; implement search-within-channel and better filters.

Overall Recommendation: Simplify the interface for newcomers, clarify terminology, and offer guided onboarding to make Twitch more accessible while retaining power-user efficiency.
