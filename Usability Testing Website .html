Welcome to Dylan Lu’s Usability Testing Project for Twitch! This site documents every phase of the usability process applied to Twitch, a global live-streaming platform known for gaming content, creative arts, and interactive chat. Through heuristic evaluation, persona creation, scenario testing, moderated sessions, and detailed findings, you’ll learn how to conduct professional UX testing end-to-end.

About Twitch: Twitch is a vibrant community where viewers watch live broadcasts, chat in real-time, support streamers through follows and paid subscriptions, and enjoy a wide range of interactive features including emotes, Bits, and channel raids. With over 240 million monthly active users, Twitch presents a wealth of opportunities to explore usability challenges such as discoverability, engagement flows, and interface clarity.

Heuristic Evaluation 🔍

We conducted an expert review using Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics to compare Twitch’s interface against ideal usability practices. Below, each heuristic violation is shown alongside a contrasting best practice:

  • Visibility of System Status:
    Issue: Search icon alone; field hidden until clicked.
    Ideal: Always-visible search bar with placeholder text (Search games, channels…), giving immediate feedback.
  • Match Between System and the Real World:
    Issue: “Subscribe” label implies free action but triggers payment flow; “Follow” icon less prominent.
    Ideal: Label “Subscribe (Paid)” and “Follow (Free)” with info tooltips explaining differences.
  • User Control & Freedom:
    Issue: No breadcrumbs or back navigation within deep pages.
    Ideal: Persistent “Home” link or breadcrumb trail to return to previous hierarchy.
  • Consistency & Standards:
    Issue: Inconsistent iconography and terminology across sections.
    Ideal: Standardize labels (e.g., always use “Browse” for category navigation) and reuse icons consistently.
  • Error Prevention:
    Issue: Instant chat prompt blocks flow without forewarning.
    Ideal: Pre-notify about login requirement before chat tasks; allow guest preview of chat.
  • Aesthetic & Minimalist Design:
    Issue: Overloaded homepage with carousels, featured streams, and sidebars.
    Ideal: Simplified default layout for new visitors; progressively disclose advanced features.
  • Recognition Rather Than Recall:
    Issue: Jargon like “Bits,” “Raids” appear without context.
    Ideal: Inline tooltips or a help overlay explaining platform-specific terms.
  • Flexibility & Efficiency of Use:
    Issue: No keyboard shortcuts or saved filter presets.
    Ideal: Implement shortcuts (e.g., press “/” to focus search) and allow saving favorite filters.
  • Error Recovery:
    Issue: Playback failures display generic errors with no guidance.
    Ideal: Provide actionable steps (e.g., “Reload,” “Back to Home”) when streams fail.
  • Help & Documentation:
    Issue: Support links buried in footer.
    Ideal: Contextual “?” icons near complex features linking to concise documentation.

Personas 👥

Based on observed behaviors in our sessions, we distilled two representative user archetypes to guide our design hypotheses:

The Overwhelmed Newcomer

Profile: Web‑literate but new to live‑stream platforms
Goals: Quickly find and watch relevant streams
Frustrations: Impulsive autoplay, hidden navigation, jargon
“I felt thrown into a video I didn’t request and had no idea where to go.”

The Methodical Explorer

Profile: Occasional live‑stream viewer exploring new content
Goals: Filter by topic or video length before committing to watch
Frustrations: Vague thumbnails, lack of tags, excessive choices
“I need concise previews or tags so I don’t waste time on irrelevant streams.”

Scenarios 📖

These scenarios represent realistic journeys users take on Twitch, focusing on their motivations, context, and key tasks. We use them to frame test activities and understand how design supports user goals.

Scenario: First‑Time Stream Discovery

Context: A newcomer visits Twitch after hearing about it from friends.
Goal: Find and watch a live Minecraft stream without prior knowledge.
Steps:

  1. Land on homepage; scan for search or category options.
  2. Enter “Minecraft” in search or navigate via Browse menu.
  3. Select a live stream; observe autoplay and initial controls.
  4. Attempt to chat or follow; note clarity of prompts.

Scenario: Topic‑Driven Browsing

Context: An occasional viewer seeks content beyond gaming.
Goal: Locate music streams or creative arts without trial‑and‑error.
Steps:

  1. Use top navigation or dropdowns to select “Music” or “Creative” categories.
  2. Scan thumbnails and titles; filter results by live vs recorded.
  3. Hover for quick previews or tags; choose a relevant stream.
  4. Explore channel’s Videos section for past performances.

Scenario: Power‑User Engagement

Context: A seasoned Twitch user wants to support favorite streamer.
Goal: Subscribe, chat actively, and access VODs efficiently.
Steps:

  1. Log in, locate the followed streamer via dashboard or search.
  2. Click “Subscribe”; navigate through payment or Prime option.
  3. Send chat message; verify moderation controls and emotes.
  4. Quickly open Past Broadcasts; use filters to find yesterday’s session.

Moderator Script 🎙️

Use this script verbatim to ensure consistency across all sessions. Adjust pronouns/names as needed.

  1. Opening & Ethical Consent:

    “Hello! Thank you for joining today’s usability study of the Twitch website. My name is Dylan. We’re here to evaluate the site’s usability—not to test you—so please feel comfortable sharing any feedback. With your permission, I’ll record audio and screen activity strictly for analysis. Your responses will remain confidential.”

  2. Think‑Aloud Prompt:

    “As you complete tasks, please narrate your thoughts out loud. For example, you might say ‘I’m looking for the search bar because I want to find Minecraft streams.’ If you fall silent, I’ll gently prompt you to keep talking.”

  3. Task 1 — Homepage Exploration:

    “Please scroll through the homepage and describe anything that feels confusing, overwhelming, or unexpected. Take your time and tell me what you notice.”

  4. Task 2 — Interest Discovery:

    “Which section or element on this page catches your interest most? If you were curious about one category (e.g., music, IRL, gaming), what would you explore first and why?”

  5. Task 3 — Search & Selection:

    “Use the search function to look for ‘Minecraft’. Describe each step: how you locate the search, what you type, how you interpret the results, and which result you choose.”

    Follow‑up if silent: “What made you decide to click that specific result?”

  6. Task 4 — Live Playback & VOD Navigation:

    “Play the live stream you’ve selected. Once it’s playing, find the ‘Videos’ or ‘Past Broadcasts’ section to access recorded content. Describe any interface elements that guide or hinder you.”

    Probe: “How easy was it to switch between live and recorded streams?”

  7. Task 5 — Interaction Flow:

    “Attempt to send a chat message and to follow or subscribe to this channel. Note any prompts, errors, or uncertainties you encounter.”

    Probe: “What did you expect to happen when you clicked ‘Subscribe’?”

  8. Debrief & Closing:

    “Thank you! Now a few quick questions: On a scale of 1 (very difficult) to 5 (very easy), how would you rate your experience? What was the most confusing part? Any suggestions for improvement? Would you return?”

    “That concludes our session. I appreciate your time and insights—they help shape better user experiences!”

Summary 📋

This comprehensive study combined an expert heuristic evaluation with live moderated sessions from two first‑time/intermediate users and one power user. Key findings include:

  • Discoverability Challenges: New users struggled to find search and category filters, often missing key navigation elements, resulting in frustration and early abandonment.
  • Clarity & Labeling: Terminology like “Subscribe” vs. “Follow” and unlabeled icons caused confusion across user types; intermediate users suggested explicit labels and in‑context tooltips.
  • Information Overload: The dense homepage and lack of a simplified first‑time view overwhelmed participants; power users operated smoothly but endorsed optional onboarding overlays.
  • Content Filtering Needs: Both novices and intermediates prized tagging and length indicators to make informed viewing choices; untagged long videos deter engagement.
  • Engagement Flows: Chat and subscription flows were functional but lacked pre‑task guidance; a brief pre‑chat prompt and clearer subscription distinctions would improve the process.
  • VOD Accessibility: Finding past broadcasts required deep navigation; enhanced filtering or a search‑within‑channel feature was recommended.

Overall: While Twitch’s robust feature set caters well to seasoned users, novices face steep learning curves. A blend of UI simplification, labeling clarity, and guided onboarding can bridge gaps, making Twitch more welcoming and efficient for all.

Top Recommendations:

  1. Improve Discoverability: Always-visible search bar with placeholder text and a prominent “Browse” menu.
  2. Clarify Labels: Explicitly annotate free vs. paid actions (e.g., “Follow (Free)”, “Subscribe (Paid)”) with tooltips.
  3. Offer Optional Onboarding: A dismissible walkthrough overlay highlighting core features for first‑time visitors.
